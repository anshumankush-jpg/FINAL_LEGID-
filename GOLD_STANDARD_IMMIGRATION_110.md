This situation is unsettling, but it follows a pattern that border and immigration 
officers deal with frequently, and it's important to understand how they actually

First, the legal reality: a withdrawn assault charge does not make you inadmissible 
to the United States under immigration law. Withdrawn means the Crown decided not 
to prosecute ‚Äî you weren't convicted, and there's no finding of guilt.

But here's what creates the actual problem at the border: Canadian and US systems 
share police records, not just convictions. When you cross into the US, Customs and 
Border Protection (CBP) officers can see arrests and charges, including withdrawn ones. 
They're not bound by Canadian court outcomes.

What CBP officers are assessing is not whether you broke the law ‚Äî they're assessing 
credibility and risk. If they ask whether you've ever been arrested or charged, and 
you say no, and their system shows the assault charge, they will view that as deception. 
That creates a credibility flag that can follow you for years, regardless of the 
withdrawn status.

The most common mistake people make: treating "withdrawn" as if it never happened. 
It did happen ‚Äî it's in police records, it's in RCMP databases, and US border systems 
can access it.

The second mistake: not understanding that border questioning is different from 
courtroom testimony. Officers have extremely broad discretion. They can deny entry 
based on credibility concerns alone, even if you're legally admissible.

What matters at the border:

- Consistency: If asked whether you've been arrested or charged, a truthful answer 
  is "yes, but it was withdrawn." Lying or omitting creates bigger problems than 
  the withdrawn charge itself.

- Documentation: Carry proof of the withdrawal (court records showing "withdrawn by Crown"). 
  Don't rely on verbal explanations.

- Tone: Brief, factual answers. Don't volunteer details, but don't evade direct questions.

The difference between CBSA (Canadian) and CBP (US) officers: CBSA has less interest 
in withdrawn charges because they're internal. CBP officers operate under different 
mandates and have no obligation to treat withdrawn charges as irrelevant.

If you're questioned at length or sent to secondary inspection, it's not because 
the charge makes you inadmissible ‚Äî it's because they're verifying credibility or 
checking for patterns.

General information only ‚Äî not legal advice. For travel with any criminal history, 
even withdrawn, immigration lawyers often recommend preparing a brief written 
explanation and withdrawal proof before you travel.

what "rehabilitation" means in US immigration contexts.
